Marriage Certificate Translation for USCIS: Requirements, Cost & Turnaround

Jason R. Ehlinger·July 8, 2026·8 min read

If you're filing a green card, a family petition, or a fiancé(e) visa and your marriage certificate isn't in English, USCIS won't accept it as-is. You need a certified English translation — and getting it wrong is one of the most common reasons couples receive a Request for Evidence (RFE) that stalls their case by months.

This guide covers exactly what USCIS requires for a marriage certificate translation, whether you need notarization, what it costs, how long it takes, and how to order the right thing the first time.

Quick answer: USCIS requires a certified translation of your foreign marriage certificate — a complete English translation with a signed Certificate of Accuracy — under 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3). Notarization is not required for USCIS. A standard one-page certificate runs about $32.50 and is typically delivered in 24–48 hours.

When do you need a certified marriage certificate translation?

A marriage certificate is a "vital document" that appears in a wide range of immigration filings. You'll typically need a certified translation for:

  • Form I-130 — Petition for Alien Relative (spousal petitions)
  • Form I-485 — Application to Register Permanent Residence (adjustment of status)
  • Form I-129F — Petition for a K-1 fiancé(e), and later the marriage proof after entry
  • Consular processing — immigrant visa applications through a U.S. embassy
  • Naturalization (N-400) — when marital history must be documented
  • Name changes, spousal benefits, and derivative status for children

What USCIS actually requires

The governing rule is 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3), which states:

"Any document containing foreign language submitted to USCIS shall be accompanied by a full English language translation which the translator has certified as complete and accurate, and by the translator's certification that he or she is competent to translate from the foreign language into English."

Breaking that down, a compliant marriage certificate translation must:

  1. Be complete — every element on the certificate is translated, including seals, stamps, marginal notes, and signatures (rendered as "[Signature]" or "[Official Seal]" where not legible).
  2. Be accurate — names, dates, and places match the original exactly, transliterated consistently.
  3. Include a Certificate of Accuracy — a signed statement in which the translator attests the translation is complete and accurate and that they are competent to translate between the two languages.

Critically, the translator does not need to be ATA-certified, a licensed official, or a government body for the translation to be valid for USCIS. Any competent translator who signs the certification meets the legal standard. That said, using an ATA-certified translator materially lowers the risk of an accuracy challenge or RFE.

Do you need notarization?

No — not for USCIS. This is the single most common point of confusion. USCIS requires certified translation, and a notary adds nothing to that requirement: a notary verifies the identity of the person signing, not the accuracy of the translation.

Paying for notarization on a USCIS marriage-certificate translation is not harmful, but it's unnecessary cost and time. Standard certified translation is sufficient.

You may need notarization if the same document is going to a foreign consulate, certain courts, or an apostille filing. When in doubt, ask the institution receiving the document. (For the full distinction, see our guide to certified vs. notarized translation.)

Common mistakes that trigger an RFE

  • Submitting the original without a translation — a foreign-language document alone is not acceptable.
  • Partial translation — leaving out seals, stamps, or marginal annotations.
  • Name/date mismatches — inconsistent transliteration between the certificate and the rest of the petition.
  • Using Google Translate — machine output isn't certified and won't be accepted. (See: can you use Google Translate for official documents?)
  • Missing or incomplete certification statement — the translator's signed attestation must be attached.
An RFE for a translation problem typically adds 3–6 months to a case. A $32.50 certified translation done correctly the first time is the cheapest insurance in your entire filing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does USCIS require a certified translation of a foreign marriage certificate?

Yes. Under 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3), any foreign-language document submitted to USCIS — including a marriage certificate — must be accompanied by a full English translation that the translator certifies as complete and accurate, along with a statement that the translator is competent to translate. This applies to I-130, I-485, K-1, and other petitions.

Does my marriage certificate translation need to be notarized for USCIS?

No. USCIS requires certified translation only — not notarized translation. A signed Certificate of Accuracy meets the requirement. Notarization is sometimes required by foreign consulates, some courts, or for apostille filings, but it is not needed for a USCIS petition.

How much does marriage certificate translation cost?

A standard one-page marriage certificate is typically around $32.50, with the exact price confirmed after the document is reviewed. Rush and same-day options are available for most language pairs. Notarization, if you need it for a non-USCIS purpose, is an add-on.

Can I translate my own marriage certificate for USCIS?

USCIS does not explicitly prohibit self-translation, but it strongly discourages it: the translator must certify competence and impartiality, and a self-translated document is far more likely to draw a Request for Evidence. Using a professional certified translator avoids delays and rejections.

How long does it take?

Standard turnaround for a single marriage certificate is typically 24–48 hours. Rush (12–24 hours) and same-day service are available for common languages such as Spanish, Mandarin, Arabic, French, and Portuguese.
