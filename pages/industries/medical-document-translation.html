Common questions

Medical document translation FAQ

Will USCIS accept your medical document translations?

Yes. USCIS requires that all medical documents submitted with immigration applications be accompanied by a full English translation and a translator certification stating that the translator is competent in both languages and that the translation is accurate and complete. Our certifications meet all USCIS requirements and have been accepted across thousands of immigration applications including I-485, N-400, and Form I-693.

Do you translate medical records from foreign countries?

Yes. We translate medical records from every country in any source language into English (or from English into any target language). We're experienced with medical record formats from Mexico, India, China, Brazil, Russia, the Philippines, Vietnam, Korea, and other countries whose formats differ from US standards. We retain all clinical information regardless of format differences.

Can I get a medical document translated the same day?

Yes. Same-day medical document translation is available at $37.49/page. Orders placed before 10 AM Eastern are typically delivered the same business day. For urgent needs outside business hours, contact us directly at sales@taikatranslations.com — we handle after-hours medical emergencies for established clients.

Does the translation need to be notarized?

Notarization is not required by USCIS for medical document translations — a translator certification is sufficient. However, some courts, insurance companies, and foreign governments require notarization. We offer notarized medical translation for an additional $40. If you're unsure whether notarization is required for your use case, contact us — we'll advise based on the specific institution.

What is the difference between medical translation and general translation?

Medical translation requires domain-specific knowledge that general translators lack. A fluent Spanish speaker who doesn't know what "ST-elevation myocardial infarction" means in clinical context cannot accurately translate a cardiology report — even if the words are technically correct in isolation. Our medical translators have clinical or biomedical backgrounds and understand anatomical, pharmacological, and diagnostic terminology at a practitioner level.
