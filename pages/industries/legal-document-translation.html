Legal Document Translation FAQ

Do legal documents need certified translation for court?

Yes. Federal Rule of Evidence 1003 and most state equivalents require that foreign-language documents submitted as evidence be accompanied by a certified English translation. "Certified" means the translator has signed a statement declaring the translation complete and accurate. Uncertified translations — including machine translations and informal human translations without a certificate — are generally not admissible. Our certified legal document translations include a Certificate of Accuracy formatted specifically for court submission and are accepted by federal and state courts nationwide.

What's the difference between certified and notarized legal document translation?

A certified translation includes the translator's signed Certificate of Accuracy attesting to the translation's completeness and accuracy. A notarized translation additionally involves a notary public who witnesses the translator's signature and affixes a notarial seal, verifying the identity of the signatory. Most US federal and state courts require only a certified translation. Some state probate courts, foreign consulates, and international proceedings require notarization. When in doubt about court requirements, check the local rules of the specific court or consult with the receiving clerk. We provide both services — notarization adds $40.
