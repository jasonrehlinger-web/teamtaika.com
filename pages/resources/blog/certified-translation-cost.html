How Much Does Certified Translation Cost? (2026 Pricing Guide)

Jason R. Ehlinger·June 22, 2026·7 min read

If you're searching for certified translation and trying to figure out what it should cost, you've hit a wall of vague answers. Most providers hide their prices or bury them behind a quote form. This guide gives you real 2026 numbers, explains exactly what drives the cost up or down, and helps you spot when a cheap quote is too good to be true.

Short answer: Certified translation costs between $24.99 and $60 per page in 2026, where one page = approximately 250 words. Most standard documents (birth certificates, marriage certificates, diplomas) run 1–3 pages and cost $25–$90 total. Rush delivery and notarization add to that. What Is a "Page" in Certified Translation?

Almost every translation provider prices by the page, but the definition of a page varies. The industry standard is approximately 250 words of source text. Some providers use 200 words, others 300 — always check before assuming your two-page birth certificate is two billable pages.

Dense legal or technical documents with small fonts and minimal white space often count as more pages than they appear. A one-page immigration form packed with fields may translate to 1.5 or 2 billable pages depending on word count. When in doubt, submit your document for a free word count before committing.

What Drives the Cost Up or Down

Language Pair

Common European languages (Spanish, French, Portuguese, German, Italian) are typically at or near base price because there's a larger pool of qualified translators. Less common language pairs — Amharic, Tigrinya, Pashto, Somali, or rare Asian languages — often carry a surcharge of 20–50% because qualified translators are harder to find and schedule. Most providers won't advertise this openly; it shows up in the quote.

Turnaround Time

Standard delivery is 24–48 hours for most single-document orders. Rush delivery (12–24 hours) typically adds 25–50% to the base price. Same-day delivery, when available, can double the cost. For common languages like Spanish, French, and Portuguese, same-day is usually achievable. For rare languages, even 48 hours can be a stretch.

Notarization

A notarized translation requires a notary public to witness the translator's signature on the Certificate of Accuracy. This adds a flat fee — typically $35–$75 — on top of the per-page translation cost. At Taika, notarization is a $40 flat add-on. Note: USCIS does not require notarization; some courts and foreign consulates do. See our guide on certified vs. notarized translation for the full breakdown.

Formatting Complexity

A plain birth certificate is fast to translate. A medical record with charts, tables, handwritten notes, and multiple stamps takes significantly longer to format properly. Some providers charge a desktop publishing (DTP) surcharge for documents that require layout work to match the original format.

Subject Matter Expertise

Legal, medical, and technical translations require domain-specific knowledge that general translators don't have. A patent, a clinical trial report, or a pharmaceutical label requires a specialist, and specialists cost more. Expect 20–40% higher rates for highly technical content.

Red Flags: When Cheap Translation Is Expensive

A $10/page certified translation sounds great — until USCIS rejects your green card application or a court refuses to accept the document. The hidden cost of a bad translation isn't the $10 you paid; it's the months of delay and the attorney fees to fix it.

Watch for these warning signs when comparing providers:

  • No Certificate of Accuracy included — a certified translation must include a signed certificate. If the provider doesn't mention it, ask explicitly.
  • Machine translation with a rubber stamp — some low-cost providers run your document through DeepL or Google Translate and slap a signature on it. Ask whether a human translator reviews the output.
  • No named translator or credentials — reputable providers can tell you who translated your document and what their qualifications are.
  • No revision policy — if USCIS sends an RFE citing a translation error, you need a provider who will fix it at no charge.
  • No physical address or contact number — translation is a service where accountability matters. Avoid providers with no verifiable business presence.
A certified translation that costs $25 and gets accepted is a bargain. A certified translation that costs $12 and triggers a 6-month USCIS delay is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make in an immigration case.

How to Get an Accurate Quote

The fastest way to get an accurate price is to upload your document and let the translator count the words. Most reputable providers offer free quotes with no obligation. The quote should specify the per-page rate, the page count, the turnaround time, and whether the Certificate of Accuracy is included.

If a provider quotes by word count instead of page count, convert it yourself: divide the word count by 250 and multiply by their per-page rate. The math should land in the same range.
