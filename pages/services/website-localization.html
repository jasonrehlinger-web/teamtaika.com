Website Localization FAQ

What is the difference between translation and localization?

Translation converts text from one language to another while preserving meaning. Localization adapts a product or content for a specific locale — a combination of language, culture, region, and market. For a website, localization includes translating the text but also adapting cultural references, adjusting date, time, and currency formats to local conventions, replacing images that don't resonate in the target culture, tuning the formality level to what the market expects, and optimizing for local search behavior. Translation is a component of localization, but localization is the more complete process for making a website genuinely perform in a foreign market.

How do I choose the right website localization provider?

The right localization provider understands both language and technology. Key questions to ask: Do their translators specialize in your industry? Do they understand your CMS and can they deliver in a format your developers can use? Do they include multilingual SEO, or is that a separate engagement? Do they have a quality assurance process (ideally dual-review)? Do they offer ongoing support for continuous content updates? How much does website localization cost?

Website localization cost depends on site size (word count is the most accurate measure), number of target languages, content complexity, and whether ongoing support is included. A typical corporate website of 50 pages (approximately 25,000 words) into Spanish starts around $2,000–$3,500 including cultural adaptation and SEO. Adding more languages scales linearly with some volume discount. E-commerce product catalog localization at scale is typically $0.12–$0.18 per word with translation memory applied. How do you handle right-to-left (RTL) languages like Arabic and Hebrew?

RTL localization requires both linguistic work and technical implementation. Linguistically, Arabic and Hebrew text is written and read right-to-left, which affects translation segmentation and flow. Technically, the entire page layout must mirror horizontally for RTL audiences — navigation, sidebars, icons, and content flow all reverse direction. We provide the translated and localized text content, and we flag all RTL-specific technical implementation requirements for your development team. For clients who need full technical implementation support, we can manage the front-end RTL implementation as part of the project scope.

How do you maintain consistency across a large localized website?

Consistency at scale requires three tools: a translation memory, a glossary, and a style guide. Translation memory stores every approved translation segment so that repeated content (navigation labels, recurring phrases, boilerplate disclaimers) is always translated the same way — which also reduces cost over time as memory grows. A glossary standardizes key brand and technical terms across the site. A style guide defines tone, voice, and register for each target language. We build and maintain all three for every localization program, and the glossary and style guide are the client's property to use beyond our engagement.
