Birth Certificate Translation for USCIS: What's Required, What It Costs, and How to Avoid Rejection

USCIS requires a certified translation of every foreign-language birth certificate submitted with an immigration petition. One missing element — or one wrong name transliteration — can result in a Request for Evidence (RFE) that delays your case by months. Here's exactly what's required and how to get it right.

What USCIS Requires Under 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3)

The controlling regulation is 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3), which states: "Any document containing foreign language submitted to USCIS shall be accompanied by a full English language translation which the translator has certified as complete and accurate, and by the translator's certification that he or she is competent to translate from the foreign language into English."

That certification is not a formality — it's a required legal statement that must appear on or with the translation. USCIS adjudicators check for it. Missing or inadequate certifications are one of the top causes of translation-related RFEs.

A compliant certification statement must include:

  • The translator's full legal name and mailing address
  • The language pair (e.g., "from Spanish to English")
  • A statement of competency: "I, [full name], certify that I am competent to translate [source language] into English"
  • A statement of accuracy: "I certify that the above translation is accurate and complete to the best of my knowledge and ability"
  • The translator's handwritten or electronic signature
  • The date the certification was signed

Note what is not required: a notary seal, ATA certification, or any government-issued credential. USCIS requires a competency and accuracy statement — not a license. That said, professional-quality translation is critical, because errors in the body of the translation are the other leading cause of RFEs.

The Most Common Reason Birth Certificate Translations Get Rejected

The single most common certification error is a vague or incomplete statement — phrases like "I am a professional translator" or "this document has been translated by a qualified expert" don't satisfy 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3) because they don't include both the competency assertion and the accuracy assertion as distinct statements.

The second most common error: translators who certify the first page of a multi-page birth certificate and leave the second and third pages uncertified. USCIS requires the certification to cover every page of the document. If your birth certificate is two pages, each page's translation must be certified — or a single certification must explicitly state it covers the complete document including all pages.

A third common problem is name inconsistency. The name as rendered in the English translation must match the name used on every other document in the petition — particularly the I-485, I-130, or N-400 itself. If your petition uses the name "Maria Elena Garcia" but the translation renders it "Maria García," that discrepancy can generate an RFE asking for clarification of identity.

What's in a Birth Certificate — and What Must Be Translated

A complete translation must render every element of the original document into English. For birth certificates, this typically includes:

  • The registrar's name, title, and official position
  • The child's full name (transliterated carefully for non-Latin scripts)
  • Both parents' full names, including mother's maiden name where indicated
  • Date and place of birth — town, municipality, state, and country
  • Civil registry book number, folio, and act or registration number
  • Description of the official seal (e.g., "Official Seal of the Civil Registry of Guadalajara, Jalisco")
  • Witness names and roles, if present
  • Marginalia or handwritten notes — these are part of the document and must be translated

For documents in non-Latin scripts — Arabic, Chinese, Hindi, Russian, Korean — name transliteration requires particular care. The rendered English spelling of the name must match the way that name is spelled everywhere else in the immigration file. A professional translator will ask about this upfront; an inexperienced one may not.

Which USCIS Forms Require Birth Certificate Translations

Birth certificate translations are required across a wide range of immigration applications. The most common:

  • I-485 (Application to Register Permanent Residence / Adjustment of Status) — Required for the primary applicant and every derivative co-applicant (spouse and children adjusting concurrently)
  • N-400 (Application for Naturalization) — Required to establish identity and date/place of birth
  • I-130 (Petition for Alien Relative) — Submitted with evidence of the qualifying family relationship, which typically requires translated birth certificates of both the petitioner and the beneficiary
  • DS-260 / DS-230 (Immigrant Visa Application) — Required at the consular interview stage for all immigrant visa applicants at U.S. embassies and consulates abroad
  • I-589 (Application for Asylum and Withholding of Removal) — Required for the applicant and any derivatives included in the application
  • I-539 (Application to Extend/Change Nonimmigrant Status) — Required when dependents are included and birth certificates are submitted as relationship evidence

If you're assembling a complex packet — for example, a principal I-485 with concurrent I-485 filings for a spouse and two children, each born in a different country — you may need translations from three or four different language combinations in a single submission.

Country-Specific Considerations

Birth certificate formats vary significantly by country — and in some cases, by state, region, and decade. Here are the most common challenge cases:

Mexico

Mexican birth certificates issued before 2003 are often handwritten civil registry entries — not printed forms. These can be difficult to read and require translators with specific experience in Mexican civil registry notation. Birth certificates issued after the 2003 national standardization effort are printed and more straightforward, but format still varies by state (a Jalisco certificate looks different from one issued in Chiapas). The CURP (Clave Única de Registro de Población) number, where present, should be included in the translation.

Dominican Republic

Dominican birth certificates often include multiple generations of family registry data — grandparents' names, prior civil registry entries, and margin annotations recording subsequent life events. These must all be translated. The Junta Central Electoral has issued modernized versions since 2014, but many applicants still hold older handwritten documents.

China

Chinese birth certificates (出生医学证明) use simplified characters. Name rendering requires care: pinyin romanization is the modern standard, but older documents may have names that were historically romanized in Wade-Giles or other systems. If an applicant has used a Wade-Giles romanization on earlier immigration documents, the translation must be consistent with that prior usage — not automatically converted to pinyin.

India

India does not have a uniform national birth certificate format. Certificates vary by state (Maharashtra vs. Tamil Nadu vs. West Bengal use entirely different formats), by municipal corporation vs. rural registry, and significantly by decade. Documents from the 1970s and 1980s may be partially in English already but include regional language elements. Documents from Kerala may be in Malayalam; from Punjab in Punjabi; from Tamil Nadu in Tamil. Each requires a translator competent in that specific regional language, not just "Hindi."
